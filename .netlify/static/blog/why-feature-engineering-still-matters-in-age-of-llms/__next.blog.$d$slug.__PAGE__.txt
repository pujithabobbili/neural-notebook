9:T752,<h2>The Hype vs. The Reality</h2>
<p>Large Language Models have captured every headline, and for good reason — they're transformative. But beneath the dazzling demos, most production ML systems still rely on classical, tabular models where <strong>feature engineering is king</strong>.</p>
<h3>The 80/20 Rule Still Applies</h3>
<p>In my experience shipping models across healthcare, fintech, and e-commerce, the single most impactful thing you can do is spend time understanding your data and building meaningful features. A well-crafted ratio or lag feature often beats adding another transformer layer.</p>
<h3>When LLMs Don't Help</h3>
<ul><li><strong>Structured / tabular data</strong>: XGBoost with great features still dominates Kaggle and production.</li>
<li><strong>Low-latency requirements</strong>: You can't always afford a 500ms inference call.</li>
<li><strong>Small data regimes</strong>: Feature engineering lets you inject domain knowledge when you have <10K rows.</li>
</ul>
<h3>A Practical Example</h3>
<p>Imagine predicting customer churn. Raw columns give you signup date and last login. But <em>engineered features</em> like "days since last login", "login frequency trend over 30 days", and "ratio of support tickets to orders" carry far more predictive signal.</p>
<pre><code>df[&quot;days_since_last_login&quot;] = (pd.Timestamp.now() - df[&quot;last_login&quot;]).dt.days
df[&quot;login_trend_30d&quot;] = df.groupby(&quot;user_id&quot;)[&quot;login&quot;].transform(
    lambda x: x.rolling(30).mean()
)
df[&quot;ticket_order_ratio&quot;] = df[&quot;support_tickets&quot;] / (df[&quot;orders&quot;] + 1)</code></pre>
<h3>The Bottom Line</h3>
<p>LLMs are incredible tools, but they haven't replaced the fundamentals.
