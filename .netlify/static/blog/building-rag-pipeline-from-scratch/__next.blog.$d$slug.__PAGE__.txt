4:T786,<h2>What is RAG, Really?</h2>
<p>Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) combines a retrieval system (usually vector search) with a generative LLM. The retriever finds relevant documents, and the LLM synthesizes an answer grounded in those documents.</p>
<h3>The Architecture</h3>
<p>At a high level, a RAG pipeline looks like this:</p>
<li><strong>Ingest</strong> — chunk your documents and embed them into a vector store</li>
<li><strong>Retrieve</strong> — given a user query, find the top-K most similar chunks</li>
<li><strong>Generate</strong> — feed those chunks + the query into an LLM for a grounded answer</li>
<h3>Mistakes I Made (So You Don't Have To)</h3>
<p><strong>Chunk size matters enormously.</strong> Too small and you lose context. Too large and you dilute the signal. I settled on ~500 tokens with 50-token overlap after extensive experimentation.</p>
<p><strong>Don't skip reranking.</strong> Vector similarity is a rough proxy. Adding a cross-encoder reranker after retrieval improved answer quality by ~30% in my benchmarks.</p>
<p><strong>Metadata filtering is underrated.</strong> If your user asks about "Q1 2026 revenue", you should filter by date metadata <em>before</em> vector search, not just rely on semantic similarity.</p>
<h3>My Recommended Stack</h3>
<ul><li><strong>Embeddings</strong>: OpenAI <code>text-embedding-3-large</code> or open-source <code>BGE-M3</code></li>
<li><strong>Vector store</strong>: Pinecone or pgvector (if you want to stay in Postgres)</li>
<li><strong>Reranker</strong>: Cohere Rerank or a fine-tuned cross-encoder</li>
<li><strong>LLM</strong>: GPT-4o or Claude for generation</li>
</ul>
<h3>Final Thoughts</h3>
<p>RAG is not a silver bullet. It works best when your documents are well-structured, your chunking is thoughtful, and you evaluate rigorously.
